# Communism, Religion, and Governance in Pakistan

## Introduction

The political history and government structure of Pakistan have been deeply shaped by two major forces: the fight between **Communism and Islamic Nationalism**, and internal **religious differences** showing up as sectarianism and religious politics. These two forces have influenced Pakistan's local policies, foreign relations, military actions, and the very nature of government in the country.

Unlike most states where challenges come from outside, Pakistan has faced these challenges **from within**, creating deep tensions between:
- **Secular/Socialist ideas** vs. **Islamic ideas**
- **Class-based movements** vs. **Religious movements**
- **International communism** vs. **National Islamic identity**
- **Sunni majority** vs. **Shia and other minorities**

Understanding these forces is needed to understand Pakistan's government challenges and political instability.

---

## Part I: The Role of Communism in Pakistan

### Historical Context: Pakistan and the Cold War

Pakistan emerged in 1947 at the dawn of the **Cold War** with a strategic choice:
- Aligned with the **Western Bloc** (United States)
- Positioned against the **Soviet Bloc**
- Adopted **Capitalism** over **Communism**

This choice was influenced by:
1. **Geopolitical**: Soviet proximity through Central Asia; US far away
2. **Ideological**: Founder Quaid-e-Azam envisioned Islamic state, not communist
3. **Religious**: Islam's opposition to atheistic materialism
4. **Economic**: Western aid and investment needed post-partition

### Early Suppression: The Rawalpindi Conspiracy (1951)

#### **The Conspiracy**

The first major incident showing state fear of communism was the **Rawalpindi Conspiracy Case of 1951**.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Leaders** | Major General Akbar Khan, Faiz Ahmed Faiz (poet), Sajjad Zaheer |
| **Goal** | Overthrow PM Liaquat Ali Khan |
| **Motivation** | Opposition to pro-Western foreign policy and Kashmir war handling |
| **Ideology** | Blend of military dissent and leftist ideology |
| **Outcome** | Conspirators arrested; severe crackdown on left |

#### **Why It Mattered**

The Rawalpindi Conspiracy set an important example:
- Communism labeled as **"anti-state"** and **"anti-Islamic"**
- State began **organized suppression** of leftist movements
- Military gained role as **protector against communist entry**
- Gave reasons for harsh measures against the left
- Fixed the story: **Communist = Enemy of Pakistan**

:::warning State Narrative Solidified
The conspiracy allowed military and religious leadership to frame communism as:
- A foreign ideology (Soviet)
- Against Islam
- Threatening national security
- A legitimate target for suppression

This narrative persisted throughout Pakistan's history.
:::

### 1954 Ban: Communist Party of Pakistan Outlawed

#### **The Ban**

In **1954**, following the Rawalpindi Conspiracy aftermath, the **Communist Party of Pakistan (CPP)** was officially banned by the government.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Official Justification** | Alleged plots against government and state security |
| **Real Reason** | Fear of communist infiltration; Cold War ideology |
| **Method** | Constitutional suppression under preventive detention laws |
| **Duration** | Long-term ban; underground activities persisted |

### The 1960s and 1970s: Underground Pressure

Despite the ban, leftist movements pressured the state on several issues:

**Issues Pushed by Leftist Movements:**
- **Land Reforms**: Breaking up landlord power structures
- **Labor Rights**: Minimum wages, better working conditions
- **Worker Organization**: Union rights and collective bargaining
- **Class Consciousness**: Highlighting wealth inequality
- **Anti-Feudalism**: Criticizing feudal power in politics

:::note Pressure from Underground
Though the organized Communist Party was banned, leftist pressure contributed to:
- Land reform initiatives
- Labor legislation
- Socialist-leaning policies in some periods
- Challenge to feudal power structures

This shows that even suppressed ideologies can influence policy through grassroots pressure.
:::

### The 1970s: "Islamic Socialism" - An Attempt to Bridge Ideologies

#### **Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's Hybrid Approach**

When **Zulfikar Ali Bhutto** came to power in 1971, he attempted something unique: merging socialism with Islam.

#### **"Islamic Socialism" Framework**

| Economic Policy | Details |
|-----------------|---------|
| **Nationalization** | Took over industries (steel, sugar, banks) |
| **Land Reform** | Redistributed land; abolished landlord privileges |
| **Labor Rights** | Implemented labor protection laws |
| **Welfare State** | Provided public education, health services |
| **Ideological Frame** | Claimed all reforms were based on Islamic justice |

#### **Quranic Justification**

Bhutto argued that Islamic socialism was rooted in:

| Principle | Quranic Basis |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Equality** | "All humans are equal as the teeth of a comb" (Hadith) |
| **Social Justice** | Prohibition of interest, emphasis on Zakat |
| **Communal Property** | Islamic concept of collective welfare |
| **Worker Rights** | Islamic emphasis on fair wages and labor dignity |

:::info Bridging Ideology
Bhutto's "Islamic Socialism" was an attempt to:
- Accept socialist economic principles (popular among youth)
- Frame them as Islamic (acceptable to traditional society)
- Modernize while remaining within Islamic framework
- Appeal to both leftists and religious constituencies

This was a creative but ultimately fragile compromise.
:::

#### **Opposition from Ulema (Religious Scholars)**

Despite Bhutto's framing, religious scholars fiercely opposed:

| Objection | Religious Scholar Argument |
|-----------|----------------|
| **"Socialism Denies God"** | Marxism clearly denies God; cannot work with Islam |
| **"Communism is Kufr" (Disbelief)** | Issued religious rulings saying socialism is un-Islamic |
| **"Anti-Religious"** | Class struggle idea ignores spiritual side |
| **"Western Import"** | Socialism is foreign idea; Islam has its own system |

**Result**: Religious parties and scholars launched massive campaign against Bhutto's policies, ultimately contributing to his removal from power.

### The Zia Era (1977-1988): Defeating Communism Through Islamization

#### **General Zia-ul-Haq's Strategy**

When **General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq** took power in a military coup (1977), he adopted a two-pronged strategy:

**Against Communism:**
- Declared Islam as antidote to communism
- Launched **Islamization campaign**
- Promoted religious education
- Supported Mujahideen in Afghanistan against Soviets

**Against Bhutto's Socialism:**
- Reversed nationalization policies
- Returned industries to private sector
- Reduced welfare state commitments
- Empowered traditional religious and feudal elites

**Sectarian Violence:**
- Sunni-led Mujahideen got weapons from state
- Shia groups formed counter-groups (feeling left out and threatened)
- State's support of Sunni militants increased sectarian violence
- Created dangerous example of state-backed militant groups

## Part II: The Role of Religious Differences in Governance

### Overview

While communism was consistently suppressed, **religious differences** played a different role:
- Religious mobilization sometimes supported by state
- Weaponized by political leaders for legitimacy
- Caused dramatic policy changes and constitutional amendments
- Led to extreme communal violence

### The 1974 Second Amendment: Ahmadis Declared Non-Muslim

#### **Constitutional Change Through Religious Pressure**

Despite **21 years passing**, religious pressure continued. In **1974**, under Bhutto, the **Second Amendment** was passed.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Date** | September 8, 1974 |
| **Amendment** | Added to Article 260 (Definition of Muslim) |
| **Content** | Officially declared Ahmadis as non-Muslims |
| **Method** | Constitutional amendment through National Assembly |
| **Significance** | First time Constitution explicitly excluded a group from Muslim identity |

#### **What the Amendment Said**

The amendment inserted this definition:

> "A person who does not believe in the absolute and unqualified finality of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) but believes in and acknowledges, as a religious personage, any person who appeared or appears after Muhammad for any purpose whatsoever, is not a Muslim."

:::warning Constitutional Precedent
This amendment had major implications:
- State endorsing religious interpretation of Muslim identity
- Constitutional redefinition based on religious orthodoxy
- Set precedent for using Constitution for religious objectives
- Undermined secular aspects of Constitution
- Demonstrated power of religious mobilization to change fundamental law

This represented **"constitutionalization of religious orthodoxy"**.
:::

#### **Impact on Ahmadis**

The amendment led to:
- **Legal Discrimination**: Ahmadis stopped from calling themselves Muslims
- **Job Restrictions**: Stopped from certain government jobs
- **Religious Restrictions**: Cannot spread faith or use Islamic words
- **Social Stigma**: Increased community discrimination
- **International Concern**: Raised human rights questions

### The Zia Era (1977-1988): Religion in Governance

#### **Islamization as Governance Strategy**

Zia used **religion as a fundamental governance tool**:

| Policy | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Shariat Courts** | Created Federal Shariat Court to test laws against Islam |
| **Islamic Curriculum** | Mandated Islamic education in schools |
| **Hudood Laws** | Introduced Islamic criminal laws (controversial) |
| **Islamic Banking** | Implemented interest-free banking system |
| **Friday Holidays** | Made Friday official holiday for congregational prayer |
| **Dress Code** | Encouraged modest Islamic dress in public institutions |

:::info Religious Governance Approach
Zia's strategy was to legitimize military rule through:
- Associating military with Islamic values
- Positioning military as defender of Islam
- Framing development as Islamization
- Using religion to suppress opposition

This created a model where military dictatorship claimed Islamic legitimacy.
:::

| Rule | Effect |
|-----------|--------|

Despite legal challenges, ordinance remains in effect.

## Part IV: Comparative Impact - Communism vs. Religion

The suppression of communism and elevation of religion created an asymmetry:

| Factor | Communism | Religion |

| **Mobilization** | Limited (suppressed) | Widespread (encouraged) |
| **Institutional Power** | Excluded from power | Inserted into governance |
| **Violence Associated** | Limited (suppressed) | Sectarian violence flourished |
| **Constitutional Impact** | Little formal change | Major constitutional amendments |
| **International Alignment** | Against US interest | With US/Saudi/Western interest |

:::note Ironic Result
By suppressing communism and promoting religion, Pakistan's governance:
- Weakened secular/socialist countervailing forces
- Empowered religious extremism
- Created sectarian divisions
- Allowed religious parties to dominate
- Weakened democratic institutions (religious parties often anti-democratic)

The irony: avoiding communist "threat" may have harmed democratic development.

## Part V: Governance Implications and Lessons

### How Ideology Shapes Governance

Pakistan's experience shows how ideological choices profoundly shape governance:

#### **1. Security Threats Shape Institutional Development**

| Period | Threat | Response | Consequence |
|--------|--------|----------|------------|
| **1947-1954** | Communist infiltration | Military intervention normalized | Military became political force |
| **1979-1989** | Soviet communism | Islamic mobilization | Religion entered governance |
| **1980s-2000s** | Sectarian violence | Military crackdowns | Centralized authority |

#### **2. Religious Politicization Weakens Democracy**

- Religious mobilization on non-constitutional issues (Ahmadi identity)
- Street violence can overturn constitutional order
- Encourages military intervention (as protector of Islam)
- Weakens rule of law (religious demands override law)

#### **3. Suppression Without Alternative Weakens Development**

- Suppressing communism without socialist alternative
- Weakened pressure for land reform, labor rights, economic justice
- Feudal and capitalist elites maintained power
- Inequality persisted and increased

### Lessons for Democratic Governance

**What Pakistan's Experience Teaches:**

1. **Ideological Foundation Matters**: How a state defines itself (Islamic vs. Secular) profoundly shapes its governance

2. **Security Concerns Can Override Rights**: Fear of communism justified suppression of civil liberties

3. **Religious Politicization Destabilizes**: Using religion for political legitimacy can trigger sectarian violence

4. **Balance is Crucial**: Completely suppressing one ideology without balancing forces is dangerous

5. **Institutional Independence is Vital**: When military or religious groups dominate, rule of law suffers

---

## Conclusion

The interaction of **Communism and Religion** in Pakistan's governance reveals crucial truths:

1. **Ideologies Matter**: How states define their ideological identity shapes policies, institutions, and stability

2. **Double-Edged Sword**: While suppressing communism prevented one threat, promoting religion without limits created new threats

3. **Sectarianism as Governance Tool**: Using religion to legitimize governance leads to sectarian violence

4. **Trade-offs Exist**: Pakistan chose to:
   - Eliminate communist threat BUT weaken secular countervailing forces
   - Strengthen Islamic identity BUT empower religious extremism
   - Support Sunni movements BUT create sectarian violence

5. **Democratic Health Requires Balance**:
   - Check on religious extremism (secular/rational voice)
   - Check on authoritarian government (civil society)
   - Inclusive governance (not excluding groups)
   - Rule of law (above ideological concerns)

As Pakistan continues to develop, these lessons remain relevant. The challenge is:
- **Strengthen democratic institutions** (not military or religious authority)
- **Protect minority rights** (esp. Ahmadis and sectarian minorities)
- **Decentralize power** (allow regional/local autonomy)
- **Emphasize rule of law** (over ideological governance)
- **Create space for diverse ideologies** (communism, socialism, capitalism, religious conservatism)
