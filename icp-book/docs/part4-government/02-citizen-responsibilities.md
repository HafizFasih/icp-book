# Rights and Responsibilities of Pakistani Citizens

## Introduction

The **1973 Constitution of the Islamic Republic of Pakistan** creates a complete system that gives citizens important rights while also setting basic duties. The Constitution is not just a paper that protects personal freedom; it also explains the **relationship between the person and the state** through a clear "social contract."

This balance between rights and responsibilities is needed for national stability, democratic rule, and social unity. **Article 5** is very important as it sets the basic duties of every citizen, while **Articles 8-28** list the basic rights. Understanding both sides is key to knowing how the Pakistani state works and what it asks of its citizens.

---

## Part I: Fundamental Rights of Citizens

### Overview

**Chapter 1, Part II (Articles 8-28)** of the 1973 Constitution lists the **Basic Rights** of all citizens and people within Pakistan's borders. These rights can be enforced and are protected by the courts, especially the Supreme Court through special court orders.

#### **Right to Life (Article 8)**

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Statement** | "No person shall be deprived of life or liberty save in accordance with law" |
| **Significance** | Most fundamental right; protects physical safety and bodily integrity |
| **Limitations** | Can be restricted only by law and through due process |
| **Scope** | Applies to all persons, not just citizens |
| **Protection** | Supreme Court enforces through habeas corpus writs |

:::info Right to Life Jurisprudence
The Supreme Court of Pakistan has interpreted the "right to life" broadly to include:
- Right to health and medical care
- Right to work and livelihood
- Right to education
- Right to safe environment
- Right to protection from torture
:::

### Article 15: Freedom of Movement

Citizens have the right to move freely throughout Pakistan and to reside and settle in any part of the country. This right can be restricted only by law for reasons of national security or public order.

| Restriction Type | When Allowed |
|------------------|-------------|
| **By Law** | Parliament can pass laws restricting movement |
| **National Security** | For protection of state security |
| **Public Order** | For maintenance of public peace |
| **Foreigners** | Non-citizens can be restricted more easily |

### Article 16: Freedom of Assembly

Citizens have the right to assemble peacefully and without arms. This right is fundamental to democracy and allows citizens to:
- Hold public meetings
- Organize demonstrations
- Protest peacefully
- Unite for common causes

**Restrictions**: The state can impose reasonable restrictions for:
- National security
- Public safety
- Prevention of disease/epidemic
- Protection of property rights

:::note Peaceful Assembly Importance
Freedom of assembly is a cornerstone of democracy. It allows citizens to collectively voice grievances and advocate for change through peaceful means rather than violence.
:::

### Article 17: Freedom of Association

Citizens have the right to form associations and unions, including:
- Political parties
- Professional associations
- Labor unions
- Civil society organizations
- Community groups

**Restrictions**: Associations cannot be formed for activities:
- Against sovereignty of Pakistan
- Against national security
- Promoting violence or terrorism
- Engaged in sedition

### Article 19: Freedom of Expression

#### **Protected Freedoms**

| Freedom | Scope |
|---------|-------|
| **Speech** | Express thoughts and opinions freely |
| **Press** | Publish news, opinions, and information |
| **Media** | Use all media for expression (TV, radio, digital) |
| **Accountability** | Hold authorities accountable through criticism |

#### **Reasonable Restrictions**

The state can impose restrictions in the interest of:

| Interest | When State Can Restrict |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Sovereignty** | Speech inciting violence against state |
| **Integrity** | Seditious speech or treason |
| **Security** | National security threats |
| **Public Order** | Incitement to violence or communal riots |
| **Decency** | Obscene material |
| **Privacy** | Private reputation of individuals |

:::warning Balancing Act
While freedom of expression is fundamental, it is not absolute. Courts must balance free speech rights against legitimate state interests like national security and public order.
:::

### Article 20: Freedom of Conscience and Religion

#### **Comprehensive Religious Freedom**

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Freedom of Conscience** | Every person entitled to freedom of conscience |
| **Freedom of Religion** | Right to profess, practice, propagate religion |
| **Freedom of Worship** | Right to worship according to religious beliefs |
| **Religious Education** | Can impart religious education to children |
| **Religious Community** | Can establish religious institutions |

#### **Protection of Minority Rights**

Minorities have special protections:
- Can maintain and manage religious institutions
- Can provide religious education
- Not compelled to attend religion classes they don't follow
- Protected from religious discrimination

:::info Secular Space Within Islam
Article 20 creates a secular constitutional space where citizens of all religions‚ÄîMuslims and non-Muslims‚Äîhave protected freedoms. This reflects the Islamic principle of religious tolerance (Quran 2:256: "There is no compulsion in religion").
:::

### Article 22: Rights of Non-Muslim Citizens

Non-Muslims (Christians, Hindus, Sikhs, Ahmadis, etc.) have special protections:

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Educational Rights** | Children cannot be forced to receive Islamic religious education contrary to their faith |
| **Personal Law** | Can be governed by their own personal laws in family matters |
| **Representation** | Reserved seats in legislatures |
| **Cultural Rights** | Right to maintain culture and traditions |
| **Non-Discrimination** | Cannot be discriminated against based on religion |

### Article 23: Right to Property

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Right** | Citizens have right to hold and dispose of property |
| **Restrictions** | Can be compulsorily acquired by state for public benefit with compensation |
| **Compensation** | Must be paid at market value |
| **Constitutional** | Land reform and reforms can be done by law |

:::note Property Rights with Social Responsibility
The Islamic principle that all land ultimately belongs to Allah (God) is reflected in the ability of the state to acquire property for public benefit. However, citizens must be justly compensated.
:::

### Article 25-A: Right to Free and Compulsory Education

This right was added by the **18th Amendment (2010)** and is groundbreaking:

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Right** | Every child has right to free and compulsory education |
| **Age Group** | Children aged 5-16 years |
| **Provided By** | The state (federal or provincial government) |
| **Quality** | Education should meet minimum standards |
| **Non-Discrimination** | All children entitled regardless of gender, caste, creed |

:::success Progressive Right
Article 25-A reflects Pakistan's commitment to education as a fundamental right, recognizing that education is key to economic development, equality, and democratic participation.
:::

### Article 37-40: Social and Economic Rights

The Constitution also contains important social and economic rights (though some are aspirational):

| Article | Right | Type |

## Part II: Fundamental Duties and Responsibilities (Article 5)

### Overview and Importance

**Article 5** is unique among constitutional documents worldwide. While most constitutions focus on protecting rights, Article 5 explicitly establishes the **fundamental duties** of citizens. It serves as the "social contract" between the individual and the state.

### Article 5(1): Loyalty to the State

#### **The Duty**

*"Loyalty to the State is the basic duty of every citizen."*

#### **Nature of the Duty**

| Aspect | Explanation |
|--------|------------|
| **"Basic Duty"** | Foundational obligation; more fundamental than other duties |
| **Universal** | Applies to all citizens, regardless of class, caste, or creed |
| **Inherent** | Exists by virtue of being a Pakistani citizen |
| **Perpetual** | Applies "wherever" the person may be |

#### **Practical Implications of Loyalty**

Loyalty to the state requires citizens to:

**Positive Obligations:**
- Support the sovereignty and integrity of Pakistan
- Defend the state when national security is threatened
- Respect the constitutional order
- Contribute to national development
- Participate in national defense (if called upon)
- Pay taxes and fulfill civic duties

**Negative Obligations (What to Avoid):**
- Avoid anti-state activities and sedition
- Refrain from espionage or sharing state secrets
- Not support separatist movements
- Not collaborate with enemies of the state
- Not engage in treason or high treason
- Not undermine constitutional order

:::warning Sedition vs. Criticism
Important distinction:
- **Legitimate**: Criticizing government policies, demanding reforms, peaceful protest
- **Sedition**: Speech inciting violence, advocating overthrow of state, supporting enemies

The state cannot punish legitimate criticism as sedition.
:::

#### **Applications in Practice**

| Scenario | Duty of Loyalty Requires |
|----------|------------------------|
| **National Emergency** | Citizens must support state defense |
| **Military Service** | Serve nation when conscripted |
| **Tax Payment** | Contribute to state revenue |
| **Law Obedience** | Follow constitutional and legal order |
| **Peaceful Conduct** | Not engage in anti-state activity |

:::info Loyalty in Islamic Tradition
Islamic jurisprudence (Fiqh) emphasizes loyalty to the Islamic state and obedience to its laws as a religious duty. Article 5 incorporates this principle into Pakistani constitutional law.
:::

### Article 5(2): Obedience to the Constitution and Law

#### **The Duty**

*"Obedience to the Constitution and law is the inviolable obligation of every citizen wherever he may be and of every other person for the time being within Pakistan."*

#### **Key Terminology Explained**

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **"Cannot be broken"** | Cannot be stopped or ignored under any situation |
| **Obligation** | Required duty; not a choice |
| **"Wherever he may be"** | Pakistani citizens must follow law even when in other countries |
| **"Every other person"** | Foreigners and visitors in Pakistan must also follow Pakistani law |

#### **Scope: Citizens and Non-Citizens**

**Pakistani Citizens - Where Bound:**
- All laws when in Pakistan (obvious)
- Certain laws even when abroad:
  - Treason laws (cannot betray country)
  - Official secrets laws (cannot reveal state secrets)
  - Foreign relations laws (cannot act against Pakistan internationally)
  - Example: A Pakistani diplomat cannot leak classified documents while posted abroad

**Non-Citizens - Where Bound:**
- All laws while in Pakistan
- Foreigners must respect Pakistani law while on Pakistani soil
- Diplomatic immunity applies only to diplomats
- Tourists and visitors subject to Pakistani criminal and civil law

:::note Rule of Law Principle
Article 5(2) enshrines the **Rule of Law**‚Äîthe principle that:
- No person is above the law
- Rulers and ruled alike bound by law
- Law applies equally to all
- Government power limited by constitution and law
:::

#### **Enforcement Through Article 6**

**Article 6** provides criminal consequences for violating Article 5:

| Violation | Consequence |
|-----------|------------|
| **High Treason** | Violating loyalty to state |
| **Subversion of Constitution** | Attempting to abrogate/subvert constitution |
| **Penalty** | Death or life imprisonment |

:::warning Constitutional Safeguard
Article 6 acts as a deterrent against coups, sedition, and high treason. It provides the legal basis for prosecuting those who attempt to overthrow the constitutional order.
:::

## Part IV: Civic Responsibilities and Social Duties

Beyond Articles 5 and constitutional rights, good citizenship entails broader civic responsibilities:

### 1. Civic Participation

**Voting and Political Participation**
- Exercise the right to vote
- Participate in democratic processes
- Vote based on merit, not coercion
- Engage in peaceful political discourse

**Community Participation**
- Attend public consultations
- Participate in civil society
- Serve on juries and committees
- Contribute to community development

### 2. Social Responsibility

**Environmental Stewardship**
- Protect natural resources
- Avoid pollution
- Support sustainable development
- Respect environmental laws

**Public Safety and Order**
- Cooperate with law enforcement
- Report crimes
- Not engage in vigilantism
- Respect others' safety and property

**Community Welfare**
- Support vulnerable populations
- Participate in charitable works
- Respect diversity and minorities
- Promote social harmony

**Tax Compliance**
- Pay lawful taxes honestly
- Not evade taxation
- Support government revenue
- Enable public service delivery

**Honest Business Practices**
- Engage in legitimate commerce
- Not engage in fraud or deception
- Respect consumer rights
- Support fair market practices

**Labor Responsibility**
- Work honestly and diligently
- Respect employer rights

**Family Responsibility**
- Support and care for family members

- Respect religious beliefs of others

- Promote communal harmony

## Part V: Constitutional Safeguards for Rights

### Judicial Protection

#### **Special Court Orders (Article 184)**

The Supreme Court and High Courts have the power to issue special orders:

| Order Type | Purpose | When Used |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Habeas Corpus** | Protect right to freedom | Illegal arrest or detention |
| **Mandamus** | Force official to do their job | Official refusing to do legal duty |
| **Prohibition** | Stop illegal action | Court acting without proper power |
| **Certiorari** | Review decisions | Challenging court decisions |
| **Quo Warranto** | Question authority | Ask if official has right to their position |

#### **Public Interest Litigation (PIL)**

Any citizen can go to courts to protect:
- Basic rights of large groups
- Public interest in environment or social matters
- Rights of weak or poor people who cannot go to courts
- Constitutional protections

:::info Access to Justice
Through PIL, the courts have made justice accessible to common people. A citizen or NGO can petition the Supreme Court directly without going through lower courts if fundamental rights are violated.
:::

### Limitation on Rights

While rights are fundamental, they can be limited in certain circumstances:

#### **Article 1-A: Suspension During Emergency**

During state of emergency:
- Some fundamental rights can be suspended
- **BUT** some rights remain inviolable:
  - Right to life (Article 8)
  - Right to dignity (Article 14)
  - Protection from torture (Article 14)
  - Loyalty and law obedience duties (Article 5)
  - Right against slavery (Article 11)

#### **Reasonable Restrictions (Balancing Test)**

Courts apply a three-part test to determine if rights restriction is valid:

1. **Legitimate Aim**: State must show legitimate purpose (national security, public order, etc.)
2. **Necessary**: Restriction must be necessary (not merely convenient)
3. **Proportionate**: Benefits must outweigh harm to rights

### Constitutional Protections for Non-Muslim Citizens

Pakistan's constitution contains specific provisions protecting minorities:

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|

### Ahmadi Community - Special Context

The **Second Amendment (1974)** declared Ahmadis (followers of Mirza Ghulam Ahmad) as non-Muslims. This decision:
- Remains controversial
- Has led to legal and social discrimination
- Created special legal status different from other minorities
- Raises questions about religious liberty and inclusion

:::warning Complex Issue
The Ahmadi question represents tension between:
- Islamic orthodoxy (mainstream Muslim belief)
- Religious liberty and minority protection
- Constitutional secularism
- Pakistan's ideological identity

This remains a challenging issue in Pakistani constitutional law and society.
:::

## Conclusion

The relationship between rights and responsibilities forms the foundation of citizenship in Pakistan. The Constitution grants citizens significant freedoms and protections, but these are balanced by fundamental duties of loyalty to the state and obedience to law.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **Rights are Protected**: Citizens enjoy extensive fundamental rights enshrined in Articles 8-28
2. **Duties are Mandatory**: Citizens have binding duties under Article 5
3. **Balance is Essential**: Rights and duties must be balanced for social stability
4. **Courts are Guardians**: Judiciary protects rights through writ jurisdiction
5. **Rule of Law Prevails**: No person, however powerful, is above the law
6. **Civic Culture Matters**: Beyond constitutional duties, good citizenship requires civic participation

As Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah stated:

> "Democracy is in the blood of the Muslims. Qur'an is the greatest democracy that the world has ever known."

This reflects the vision that Pakistan's constitutional order, while modern and secular in structure, is rooted in Islamic principles of justice, equality, and consultation (Shura).

## Summary Flashcards

### Q1: What is Article 5 about?
**A:** Article 5 sets the basic duties of citizens - loyalty to the state and obedience to the Constitution and law.

### Q2: What does "inviolable obligation" mean in Article 5(2)?
**A:** It means a duty that cannot be broken, stopped, or ignored under any situation.

### Q3: Name three fundamental rights in Pakistan's Constitution.
**A:** Right to life (Article 8), freedom of expression (Article 19), and freedom of religion (Article 20).

### Q4: What is Article 25-A about?
**A:** Right to free and compulsory education for all children aged 5-16 years.

### Q5: What is the difference between loyalty and obedience?
**A:** Loyalty is emotional commitment to the state. Obedience is following the law.

### Q6: Who must obey Pakistani law?
**A:** All Pakistani citizens (even when abroad) and all foreigners while in Pakistan.

### Q7: What is Habeas Corpus?
**A:** A special court order to protect someone from illegal arrest or detention.

### Q8: What is Public Interest Litigation (PIL)?
**A:** When any citizen can go to court to protect the rights of large groups or public interest.

### Q9: Can fundamental rights be suspended?
**A:** Yes, during a state of emergency, but some rights like right to life and protection from torture can never be suspended.

### Q10: What are the two main parts of Article 5?
**A:** Article 5(1) - Loyalty to the State, and Article 5(2) - Obedience to Constitution and Law.

### Q11: What protections do religious minorities have?
**A:** Freedom of religion, right to manage their institutions, reserved seats in Parliament, and cannot be forced to study other religions.

### Q12: What is the difference between rights and duties?
**A:** Rights are things the state must provide or protect. Duties are things citizens must do.
