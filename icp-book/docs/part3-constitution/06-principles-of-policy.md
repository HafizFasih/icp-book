# Principles of Policy (Articles 29-40)

## Introduction

The **Principles of Policy** are contained in **Part II, Chapter 2** (Articles 29 to 40) of the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973. While "**Fundamental Rights**" (Chapter 1) are legally enforceable by courts, the **Principles of Policy serve as a constitutional framework and ethical guide for the state**.

They set the **ideological direction** for the government, outlining the socio-economic and political goals the state must strive to achieve. Think of them as the **"Manifesto of the State"** or the **"Aspirational Goals"** of Pakistan's governance.

### Key Distinction:

| Fundamental Rights | Principles of Policy |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Enforceable in courts** | **Not enforceable in courts** |
| Citizens can sue if violated | Citizens cannot sue for non-implementation |
| Mandatory, immediate application | Guidelines, long-term implementation |
| Protect individual liberty | Guide state policy direction |

:::note Not Enforceable in Courts but Important
Although not legally enforceable through courts, Article 29 mandates that **every organ of the state must act in accordance with these principles**. They form the constitutional conscience of the state.
:::

---

## Framework and Responsibility

### Article 29: Definition and Scope

**"The principles of policy set out in this Chapter shall be regarded as fundamental in the governance of the State."**

**Key Implications:**
- These are **fundamental to governance**, not optional
- Must guide all state policies and decisions
- **Every organ and authority** (executive, legislature, judiciary) must follow them
- Serve as **constitutional guidance** for policymaking
- Represent the **ideological commitments** of Pakistan

### Article 30: Legal Status and Not Enforceable in Courts

**"The validity of an action or law cannot be questioned in court on the ground that it violates the Principles of Policy."**

**What This Means:**
- Courts **cannot strike down laws** on grounds of violating these principles alone
- However, violations can be used as **supporting evidence** for other constitutional violations
- Protects the state from litigation while maintaining symbolic importance
- Acknowledges that implementation depends on **available resources** and circumstances

**Why Not Enforceable in Courts?**
- These are long-term, resource-dependent goals
- Depend on economic conditions and development
- Cannot be implemented overnight
- Require flexibility in timing and implementation
- Courts cannot micro-manage state resources

:::info Judicial Use
While not directly enforceable, courts have cited these principles in interpreting other rights and striking down discriminatory laws that violate them.
:::

## Detailed Explanation of Articles (29 to 40)

### A. Islamic and Social Obligations

#### Article 31: Islamic Way of Life

**"The State shall take steps to enable the Muslims of Pakistan to order their lives in accordance with the fundamental principles and basic concepts of Islam."**

**Specific Obligations:**
The state must:

1. **Make teaching of Quran and Islamiat (Islamic Studies) compulsory** in educational institutions
   - Ensures Muslim youth understand Islamic principles
   - Builds religious consciousness in society
   - Prepares citizens for Islamic living

2. **Encourage learning of Arabic language**
   - Arabic is the language of Quran and Islamic scholarship
   - Enables understanding of Islamic sources
   - Connects Pakistan to broader Muslim world

3. **Secure proper organization of:**
   - **Zakat** (Islamic alms/charity) â†’ Welfare system
   - **Ushr** (tax on produce) â†’ Agricultural welfare
   - **Auqaf** (religious endowments) â†’ Institutional support
   - **Mosques** (prayer centers) â†’ Religious infrastructure

**Islamic Economic System:**
- These mechanisms create an **Islamic welfare state**
- Zakat and Ushr redistribute wealth to poor
- Auqaf provide for education, healthcare, and social services
- Represent Islam's approach to social justice

:::note Historical Implementation
Pakistan established the Zakat and Ushr Fund (1980) to implement these principles, demonstrating the state's commitment to Article 31.
:::

### B. Rights of Women and Minorities

#### Article 34: Full Participation of Women in National Life

**"Steps shall be taken to ensure the full participation of women in all spheres of national life."**

**Areas of Participation:**
- **Political sphere** â†’ Voting, contesting elections, holding office
- **Economic sphere** â†’ Work, business, property rights
- **Social sphere** â†’ Education, healthcare, community activities
- **Administrative sphere** â†’ Government employment, judiciary
- **Educational sphere** â†’ All levels of education

**State Obligation:**
- Remove barriers to women's participation
- Provide equal opportunities
- Ensure protection against discrimination
- Support women's development and empowerment

**Implementation Examples:**
- Reserved seats for women in legislatures (60 in National Assembly)
- Women's colleges and schools
- Female judges and civil servants
- Workplace protection laws
- Educational scholarships for girls

#### Article 35: Protection of Family and Motherhood

**"The State shall protect the marriage, the family, the mother, and the child."**

**What It Protects:**
1. **Marriage:** Sacred contract and social institution
2. **Family:** Fundamental unit of society
3. **Mother:** Special protection for motherhood
4. **Child:** Safeguarding childhood and development

**State Responsibilities:**
- Family-friendly laws and policies
- Maternity protection and benefits
- Child labor prevention
- Education and healthcare for children
- Support for single mothers
- Inheritance and guardianship laws
- Custody protection

**Significance:**
- Recognizes family as fundamental social unit
- Acknowledges mother's special role
- Protects vulnerable children
- Balances individual rights with family stability

#### Article 36: Protection of Minorities

**"The State shall safeguard the legitimate rights and interests of minorities, including their due representation in Federal and Provincial services."**

**Minority Protection Includes:**
- **Religious minorities** â†’ Christians, Hindus, Sikhs, Parsis, Ahmadis (controversial)
- **Ethnic minorities** â†’ Non-dominant ethnic groups
- **Linguistic minorities** â†’ Non-Urdu speakers

**Safeguards Provided:**
1. **Representation in Services:**
   - Due representation in civil service
   - Government employment opportunities
   - Participation in administration

2. **Legitimate Rights Protection:**
   - Protection of property
   - Freedom of religion (Article 20)
   - Equal education opportunities
   - Fair legal treatment

3. **Reserved Seats:**
   - 10 seats reserved in National Assembly for minorities
   - Representation in provincial assemblies
   - Participation in democratic process

**Challenges:**
- Implementation often falls short of ideal
- Discrimination persists in practice
- Need for stronger enforcement mechanisms

:::note Interfaith Harmony
The successful protection of minorities is essential for Pakistan's stability as a multi-religious society, despite being an Islamic state.
:::

### C. Socio-Economic Justice

#### Article 37: Promotion of Social Justice and Eradication of Social Evils

**"The State shall promote the educational and economic interests of backward classes or areas and remove illiteracy."**

**Four Components:**

**1. Promote Backward Areas:**
- Development of economically backward regions
- Infrastructure investment in less developed areas
- Poverty alleviation programs

**2. Eliminate Illiteracy:**
- Free and compulsory primary education
- Adult literacy programs
- Educational infrastructure expansion
- Removing barriers to education

**3. Provide Justice:**
- Inexpensive and expeditious (speedy) justice
- Legal aid for poor
- Quick case disposal
- Accessible courts

**4. Prevent Social Evils:**
- Prohibition of prostitution
- Prevention of gambling
- Prevention of drug addiction
- Health and welfare initiatives

**Significance:**
- Targets root causes of social problems
- Education as key to development
- Justice as pillar of fair society
- Prevention better than cure

#### Article 38: Promotion of Social and Economic Well-Being

**"The State shall secure:**
- **Subsistence, basic facilities and comforts of life**
- **Preservation of health**
- **Protection from unemployment**
**to all citizens irrespective of sex, caste, creed or religion."**

This is the **most comprehensive article on welfare**. It includes:

**1. Prevention of Wealth Concentration:**
- No concentration of wealth in few hands
- Progressive taxation
- Wealth redistribution mechanisms
- Fair distribution of resources

**2. Basic Necessities for Unable:**
The state must provide to those unable to earn through infirmity or sickness:
- **Food and nutrition**
- **Clothing and shelter**
- **Education and healthcare**
- **Medical relief and welfare**

**3. Reduce Income Disparity:**
- Minimize gap between rich and poor
- Progressive economic policies
- Fair wages and working conditions
- Social safety nets

**4. Elimination of Riba (Interest):**
- **"The State shall eliminate Riba (usury) as early as possible."**
- Replace interest-based system with Islamic banking
- Introduce Shariah-compliant finance
- Promote Islamic economic models

**Significance:**
- Establishes Pakistan as a **welfare state**
- Islamic economic justice
- Social safety for vulnerable
- Economic security for all citizens

:::note Islamic Banking
Following Article 38, Pakistan established Islamic banking system with Shariah-compliant alternatives to interest-based banking, demonstrating attempt to implement this principle.
:::

### D. National and International Relations

#### Article 39: Participation of People in Armed Forces

**"The State shall endeavor to enable people from all parts of Pakistan to participate in the Armed Forces."**

**Objectives:**
- **Representative military:** Forces reflect all regions
- **Equal opportunity:** All ethnic groups represented
- **National integration:** Military as national institution
- **Professionalism:** Merit-based recruitment

**Implementation:**
- Recruitment from all provinces
- Officer training programs accessible to all
- Representation in army, navy, air force
- Training institutions spread geographically

**Significance:**
- Prevents military dominance by one region
- Builds national cohesion
- Ensures diverse representation in defense

#### Article 40: Strengthening Bonds with Muslim World

**"The State shall endeavor to preserve and strengthen fraternal relations among Muslim countries based on Islamic unity."**

Also includes: **"The State shall promote international peace and foster goodwill and friendly relations among nations."**

**Dual Objectives:**

**1. Islamic Unity:**
- Strengthen bonds with Muslim-majority nations
- Cooperate on Islamic issues
- Support Muslim causes globally
- Educational and cultural exchange with Muslim world

**2. International Peace:**
- Promote peaceful resolution of conflicts
- Cooperate with all nations
- Support international law and institutions
- Contribute to global stability

**Pakistan's Actions:**
- Member of Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC)
- Diplomatic relations with multiple countries
- International development partnerships
- Peacekeeping contributions

**Balance:**
- Islamic orientation while maintaining broader international relations
- Unique position as bridge between Islamic and Western worlds

:::note Geopolitical Positioning
Pakistan's dual commitment to Islamic solidarity and international cooperation reflects its unique position in global politics.
:::

## Implementation Challenges

While these principles are aspirational and non-justiciable, their actual implementation faces challenges:

### Obstacles to Implementation:

1. **Resource Constraints:** Limited budget for all aspirations
2. **Political Will:** Requires sustained commitment across governments
3. **Coordination:** Multiple agencies need to work in sync
4. **Socioeconomic Realities:** Poverty limits ability to provide basics
5. **Regional Tensions:** Sectarianism and ethnic conflicts hamper unity
6. **Implementation Gap:** Difference between law and practice

### Importance Despite Challenges:

- Set the **direction** for state policy
- Provide **ethical framework** for governance
- Guide **legislative and executive** decision-making
- Influence **judicial interpretation** of rights
- Inspire **social movements** toward justice
- Represent the **nation's values and aspirations**

## Comparison: Principles of Policy vs. Fundamental Rights

| Aspect | Fundamental Rights | Principles of Policy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Justiciability** | Enforceable in courts | Non-enforceable |
| **Immediate** | Must be protected now | Long-term aspirations |
| **Focus** | Individual freedom & protection | State's ethical direction |
| **Nature** | Rights to claim from state | Goals state should pursue |
| **Enforcement** | Courts can order compliance | No court enforcement |
| **Citizen Action** | Can sue state for violation | Cannot sue directly |
| **Example** | Right to free speech | Elimination of illiteracy |

## Conclusion

The **Principles of Policy (Articles 29â€“40) represent the constitutional conscience of the State of Pakistan.** While they differ from Fundamental Rights in their enforceability, they are **not merely ornamental**. They impose a **constitutional obligation** on every organ of the state to act in accordance with them.

These principles bind the state to:
- Preserve and promote Islamic values
- Ensure social and economic justice
- Protect vulnerable populations (women, children, minorities)
- Eliminate social evils and prejudices
- Work toward a welfare state
- Maintain international peace

The **spirit of these principles** guides Pakistan toward becoming a true **Islamic Welfare State**, balancing modern governance with Islamic values and social justice. Though implementation remains imperfect, these principles represent the **ideological foundation** of Pakistan's aspirations for a just, inclusive, and prosperous society.

## Flashcards for Quick Review

### 1. What are Principles of Policy and where are they found?
**Answer:** Guidelines for state policy found in Part II, Chapter 2 (Articles 29-40) of the 1973 Constitution.

### 2. What is the main difference between Fundamental Rights and Principles of Policy?
**Answer:** Fundamental Rights are enforceable in courts, while Principles of Policy are not enforceable in courts but guide state policy.

### 3. Can citizens sue the government for not implementing Principles of Policy?
**Answer:** No, these principles are not enforceable in courts, so citizens cannot directly sue for their non-implementation.

### 4. What does Article 31 require about Islamic way of life?
**Answer:** The state must make teaching of Quran and Islamiat compulsory, encourage Arabic language, and organize Zakat, Ushr, Auqaf, and Mosques.

### 5. What does Article 33 ask the state to discourage?
**Answer:** Five types of prejudices - parochial (local), racial, tribal, sectarian, and provincial.

### 6. What does Article 34 promise for women?
**Answer:** The state will take steps to ensure full participation of women in all spheres of national life.

### 7. What does Article 36 protect?
**Answer:** The rights and interests of minorities, including their representation in federal and provincial services.

### 8. What does Article 37 aim to eliminate?
**Answer:** Illiteracy, social evils like prostitution and gambling, and provide inexpensive and quick justice.

### 9. What economic goal does Article 38 include?
**Answer:** Elimination of Riba (interest/usury) as early as possible, and prevention of wealth concentration in few hands.

### 10. What does Article 40 promote?
**Answer:** Strengthening bonds with Muslim countries based on Islamic unity, and promoting international peace.

### 11. Why are Principles of Policy not enforceable in courts?
**Answer:** They are long-term, resource-dependent goals that depend on economic conditions and cannot be implemented overnight.

### 12. Despite not being enforceable, why are Principles of Policy important?
**Answer:** They set the ideological direction for the state, guide policy-making, and represent the constitutional conscience and aspirations of Pakistan.
