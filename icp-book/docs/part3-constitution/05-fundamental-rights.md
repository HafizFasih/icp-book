# Fundamental Rights (Articles 8-25)

## Introduction

**Fundamental Rights** are the basic human rights guaranteed to every citizen of a state, ensuring their liberty, dignity, and equality. In the **Constitution of Pakistan 1973**, these rights are enshrined in **Part II, Chapter 1 (Articles 8â€“28)**.

These rights are considered **"enforceable in court,"** meaning that if the state violates these rights, a citizen can approach the **High Court** (under Article 199) or the **Supreme Court** (under Article 184(3)) for enforcement. This judicial enforcement mechanism is crucialâ€”it transforms fundamental rights from merely theoretical declarations into enforceable legal protections.

The fundamental rights form the **bedrock of democracy in Pakistan**, limiting the power of the state and protecting the individual from tyranny and governmental excess.

:::success Constitutional Protection
The inclusion of court-enforceable fundamental rights ensures that citizens are not merely subjects of the state but rights-bearing individuals whose dignity and liberty are constitutionally protected.
:::

## Categorization of Fundamental Rights

The Fundamental Rights can be broadly categorized into several groups:

1. **Supremacy of Rights** (Article 8)
2. **Security and Personal Liberty** (Articles 9â€“14)
3. **Political and Civil Freedoms** (Articles 15â€“19A)
4. **Religious Freedoms** (Articles 20â€“22)
5. **Property and Equality** (Articles 23â€“25A)

Each category addresses different dimensions of human dignity and freedom.

---

## 1. Supremacy of Fundamental Rights (Article 8)

**Article 8** serves as the **protective shield** for all other rights.

### Key Provision:
**"The State shall not make any law which takes away or abridges the Fundamental Rights."**

### Significance:

- **Any law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights is void**
- The state is **prohibited** from making laws that take away or abridge these rights
- The **judiciary has the power to strike down such laws**
- Creates a **hierarchy of law**: Constitution > Fundamental Rights > Ordinary Laws

### Example:
If Parliament passes a law that violates freedom of speech or denies equality before the law, the Supreme Court can declare that law unconstitutional and void, even if Parliament enacted it with large majorities.

:::note Constitutional Supremacy
This article establishes that the Constitution, and particularly Fundamental Rights, stand above all other laws, making them the ultimate standard of legality.
:::

## 2. Security and Personal Liberty (Articles 9â€“14)

This cluster of articles protects the physical existence and dignity of citizens.

### Article 9: Security of Person

**"No person shall be deprived of life or liberty save in accordance with the law."**

This is the most **basic right to existence**. Key aspects:

- No one can be arbitrarily killed or imprisoned
- Only the law can authorize deprivation of life or liberty
- Provides foundation for all other rights
- Protects against state violence and arbitrary detention

:::critical Historical Importance
During the Partition of 1947, thousands were killed in communal violence. Article 9 ensures such violence cannot be perpetrated by the state.
:::

### Article 10: Safeguards as to Arrest and Detention

This article provides protections when a person is arrested:

**Key Protections:**
1. **Right to Know Grounds:** An arrested person **must be informed of the grounds of arrest**
2. **Right to Counsel:** The arrested person has the **right to consult a lawyer**
3. **Produce Before Magistrate:** Must be **produced before a magistrate within 24 hours**
4. **No Delayed Detention:** Cannot be detained beyond 24 hours without judicial authorization

**Procedure Safeguard:**
- This prevents "disappearances" and secret detention
- Ensures transparency in law enforcement
- Gives arrested person legal recourse

### Article 10-A: Right to Fair Trial

**Added via the 18th Amendment (2010)**, this guarantees:

- **Right to a fair trial** in all civil and criminal matters
- **Due process** must be observed
- Right to be heard and present evidence
- Right to legal representation
- Right to appeal

:::note 18th Amendment Enhancement
The 18th Amendment strengthened constitutional protections by explicitly recognizing the right to fair trial, reflecting modern standards of justice.
:::

### Article 11: Prohibition of Slavery and Forced Labor

**Slavery and human trafficking are strictly prohibited.**

**Specific Provisions:**
- **Slavery is forbidden** in all forms
- **Forced labor is prohibited**
- **Child labor banned:** Children under 14 cannot work in hazardous factories or mines
- Protects vulnerable populations from exploitation

**Modern Relevance:**
- Addresses human trafficking networks
- Protects child workers
- Ensures dignity in work relationships

### Article 12: Protection against Retrospective Punishment

**"No person shall be punished for an act which was not an offence at the time it was committed."**

Key Principles:
- **A person cannot be punished** for something that wasn't illegal when they did it
- **Laws cannot have retroactive effect** on criminal matters
- Protects against arbitrary legal changes targeting individuals
- Ensures people know the legal boundaries at the time of action

**Example:**
If tomorrow the government makes "riding a bicycle" illegal, they cannot arrest people who rode bicycles before the law was enacted.

### Article 13: Double Jeopardy and Self-Incrimination

This article protects against two injustices:

**1. Double Jeopardy:**
- **No one can be punished for the same offence twice**
- If acquitted, the person cannot be tried again for the same crime
- Ensures finality of judgments

**2. Protection against Self-Incrimination:**
- **No one can be forced to be a witness against themselves**
- A person cannot be compelled to give evidence that would incriminate them
- Right to silence is protected

### Article 14: Inviolability of Dignity of Man

**"The dignity of man and the privacy of the home are inviolable."**

**Protections:**
- **Dignity is fundamental** and cannot be violated
- **Privacy of home is protected** against arbitrary searches
- **Torture is strictly prohibited:** No torture for extracting evidence or confessions
- **Secret interrogation is illegal**

**Practical Implications:**
- Police cannot beat confessions out of suspects
- Homes cannot be searched without warrant
- Personal privacy is a constitutional right

:::critical Anti-Torture Provision
This article addresses historical abuses where police used torture to extract confessions, ensuring Pakistan complies with international human rights standards.
:::

## 3. Political and Civil Freedoms (Articles 15â€“19A)

These rights allow citizens to participate in the democratic process and society:

### Article 15: Freedom of Movement

**"Every citizen shall have the right to remain and reside in any part of the State and to move freely throughout the State."**

**Protections:**
- **Freedom to move** throughout Pakistan
- **Right to reside** anywhere in the country
- **Freedom of internal travel**
- Can only be restricted for public safety, health, or morality

**Practical Impact:**
- A Sindhi can move to Punjab, a Pashtun to Balochistan
- Prevents provincial authorities from restricting citizen movement
- Supports national unity transcending provincial boundaries

### Article 16: Freedom of Assembly

**"Every citizen shall have the right to freedom of peaceful assembly without arms."**

**Key Features:**
- **Right to gather peacefully** with others
- **Without carrying weapons**
- Can assemble for protest, meetings, celebrations
- State cannot prevent peaceful gatherings
- Can only be restricted if assembly poses threat to public order

**Democratic Significance:**
- Enables citizens to collectively voice demands
- Supports democratic participation
- Allows political rallies, protests, demonstrations

### Article 17: Freedom of Association

**"Every citizen shall have the right to form associations or unions."**

**What's Protected:**
- **Right to form unions:** Labor unions, professional associations
- **Right to form political parties**
- **Right to join voluntary organizations**
- Exceptions: Those in service of Pakistan (military, police) have restrictions

**Practical Examples:**
- Workers can form trade unions
- Citizens can form NGOs and social organizations
- Political movements can be organized
- Protects collective action for common interests

### Article 18: Freedom of Trade and Profession

**"Every citizen has the right to enter any lawful profession or trade."**

**Protections:**
- **No restrictions on occupation** based on caste, creed, or origin
- **Equal opportunity** in professions
- **State cannot arbitrarily exclude** from trades or professions
- Can only be limited by law for reasons of public interest

**Significance:**
- Prevents discrimination in employment
- Ensures economic opportunity for all citizens
- Protects merit-based selection in professions

### Article 19: Freedom of Speech and Expression

This is one of the **most important fundamental rights**:

**Basic Right:**
**"Every citizen shall have the right to freedom of speech and expression, and there shall be freedom of the press."**

**Protections:**
- **Freedom to speak** one's views
- **Freedom of the press** to publish news and opinions
- **Freedom to criticize** government policies and officials
- **Freedom to publish** books, newspapers, magazines
- **Freedom of media** to report on matters of public interest

**Reasonable Restrictions:**
- Security of Pakistan (national defense)
- Friendly relations with foreign states
- Public order and morality
- Contempt of court
- Defamation
- **Incitement to violence**
- **Blasphemy** (controversial, subject to debate)

**Democratic Importance:**
- Essential for democratic accountability
- Allows criticism of government
- Enables informed public opinion
- Protects press freedom

:::warning Balancing Act
Article 19 must be balanced with national security and social harmony. Courts must determine if speech restrictions are "reasonable" and proportionate.
:::

### Article 19-A: Right to Information

**Added via the 18th Amendment (2010):**

**"Every citizen shall have the right to have access to information in all matters of public importance subject to such reasonable restrictions as may be imposed by law."**

**Key Aspects:**
- Citizens have **right to government information**
- Applies to matters of **public importance**
- Enables informed decision-making by citizens
- Promotes government transparency
- Supports accountability mechanisms

**Practical Impact:**
- Citizens can file RTI (Right to Information) requests
- Government must disclose public documents
- Promotes anti-corruption efforts
- Enables media to investigate

## 4. Religious Freedoms (Articles 20â€“22)

Pakistan protects the rights of minorities and religious practice:

### Article 20: Freedom of Religion

**"Every citizen shall have the right to profess, practice and propagate his religion."**

**Comprehensive Protection:**
- **Right to believe** in any religion
- **Right to practice** religious rituals and customs
- **Right to propagate** one's religion
- **Religious minorities have equal rights**
- **Each religious denomination can manage its own institutions**

**Significance:**
- Protects religious minorities in an Islamic state
- Allows religious education and preaching
- Ensures plural society despite Islamic majority
- Respects diversity of faith

:::note Minority Protection
This article is particularly important in Pakistan, ensuring that the Christian, Hindu, Sikh, Parsi, and other communities can freely practice their religions.
:::

### Article 21: Safeguard against Taxation for Religion

**"No person shall be compelled to pay any special tax for the propagation of a religion other than his own."**

**Protection Guaranteed:**
- **No religious tax** can be imposed on individuals
- **Cannot be forced to support** another religion financially
- **Zakat** can be collected only from Muslims for Islamic purposes
- Protects religious conscience and autonomy

**Example:**
A Hindu cannot be forced to pay a special tax to support Islamic institutions, though general taxation applies to all.

### Article 22: Safeguards in Educational Institutions

**"No person attending an educational institution shall be required to receive religious instruction if it relates to a religion other than his own."**

**Educational Protection:**
- **Religious instruction is optional** for minorities
- **Students cannot be forced** to study other religions
- **Religious teaching must match student's faith**
- Schools must provide alternative arrangements

**Practical Application:**
- Islamic studies is mandatory for Muslim students but optional for others
- Schools provide alternative subjects for minority students
- Protects religious autonomy in education
- Ensures inclusive education

## 5. Property and Equality (Articles 23â€“25A)

### Article 23 & 24: Property Rights

**Article 23:** "Every citizen shall have the right to acquire, hold and dispose of property in any part of the State."

**Article 24:** "No person shall be deprived of property save in accordance with law."

**Protections:**
- **Right to own property** (land, buildings, goods)
- **Right to buy and sell** property freely
- **Right to inherit property**
- **Right to devise property** in a will
- **Cannot be arbitrarily seized**
- Seizure allowed only **for public purpose with compensation**

**Limitations:**
- **Compulsory acquisition** for public good (roads, schools, hospitals)
- Must provide **fair compensation**
- Must follow legal procedure
- Property acquired for genuine public purpose

:::info Landlord-Tenant Balance
These articles must be balanced with state's right to implement land reforms and prevent property concentration, as per Article 38 (Principles of Policy).
:::

### Article 25: Equality of Citizens

This is perhaps the **most fundamental and important right**:

**"All citizens are equal before the law and are entitled to equal protection of the law."**

**What This Means:**
- **Equality before the law:** All citizens have same legal status
- **Equal protection:** Laws apply equally to all
- **No discrimination:** No person can be treated differently by law
- **Merit-based treatment:** Only legitimate differences justify different treatment

### Article 25 (2): Prohibition of Discrimination

**Specific Prohibition:**
**"There shall be no discrimination on the basis of sex alone."**

**Further Added:**
**"The State shall not discriminate any person as to access in educational institutions on the ground only of religion, race, caste, sex, residence or place of birth."**

**Key Aspects:**
- **Women cannot be discriminated against** solely based on gender
- **Women cannot be denied education** based on sex
- **Access to public places** must be equal
- Though **special provisions** can be made for women's protection

**Example:**
Women cannot be denied entry to educational institutions or jobs solely because of their gender, though special protections (like safety measures) can be provided.

### Article 25-A: Right to Education

**Added via the 18th Amendment (2010):**

**"The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of five to sixteen years."**

**Constitutional Obligation:**
- **Free education** is a right, not a privilege
- **Compulsory** for all children 5-16 years old
- **State responsibility** to ensure access and enrollment
- **Quality education** must be provided
- **Removes economic barriers** to education

**Significance:**
- Makes education a fundamental right
- Shifts from charity to constitutional obligation
- Recognizes education as key to development
- Addresses poverty-driven dropout rates

:::success Social Development
This amendment recognizes that without education, other fundamental rights cannot be meaningfully exercised, making it foundational for human development.
:::

## How Fundamental Rights Are Enforced

### The Judicial Enforcement Mechanism:

**Article 199 - High Courts:**
- High Courts can issue writs to enforce fundamental rights
- Citizens can file petitions when fundamental rights are violated
- Writs available: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, Certiorari

**Article 184(3) - Supreme Court:**
- Supreme Court can directly hear cases involving fundamental rights
- If a matter of public importance exists
- Particularly for constitutional interpretation
- Can take suo moto (on its own motion) action

**Writs Explained:**

| Writ | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Habeas Corpus** | Challenge unlawful detention; demand release |
| **Mandamus** | Compel official/authority to perform duty |
| **Prohibition** | Stop unlawful actions by authorities |
| **Quo Warranto** | Challenge legitimacy of appointment/office |
| **Certiorari** | Demand records to review legality of actions |

:::note Court Access
These mechanisms ensure citizens have direct access to courts for protection of rights, making fundamental rights practically enforceable, not merely theoretical.
:::

## Exceptions and Limitations

### General Exceptions:

Most fundamental rights can be subject to "reasonable restrictions" for:
- Security of state
- Public order and morality
- Prevention of crime
- Health and safety
- Friendly relations with other states
- Contempt of court

### Important Limitation:

**Armed Forces Exception:**
- Members of armed forces and paramilitary forces have **restricted fundamental rights**
- Necessary for discipline and efficiency
- Cannot form associations, have limited free speech

## Conclusion

**Fundamental Rights (Articles 8-25) of the 1973 Constitution provide a comprehensive framework for the protection of human rights in Pakistan.** By covering aspects ranging from physical security and civil liberties to religious freedom and equality, the Constitution aims to create a **welfare state where the rule of law prevails**.

The inclusion of rights like:
- **Right to Fair Trial (10-A)**
- **Right to Information (19-A)**
- **Right to Education (25-A)**

...through amendments reflects the **evolving nature of the Constitution** to meet modern democratic standards.

**It is the duty of the judiciary** to ensure these rights are not just theoretical, but practically available to every citizen. When governments overstep or violate these rights, courts must stand as guardians of constitutional freedoms, protecting citizens from state excess.

## Flashcards for Quick Review

### 1. What are fundamental rights and where are they found in the Constitution?
**Answer:** Basic human rights guaranteed to citizens, found in Part II, Chapter 1 (Articles 8-28) of the 1973 Constitution.

### 2. What does "enforceable in court" mean for fundamental rights?
**Answer:** Citizens can go to court (High Court or Supreme Court) if their fundamental rights are violated by the state.

### 3. What does Article 8 say about fundamental rights?
**Answer:** The state cannot make any law that takes away or reduces fundamental rights. Any such law is void.

### 4. What is Article 9 about?
**Answer:** Security of person - No person can be deprived of life or liberty except according to law.

### 5. What are the four key protections in Article 10 when someone is arrested?
**Answer:** (1) Must be told reasons for arrest, (2) Right to consult a lawyer, (3) Must be produced before magistrate within 24 hours, (4) Cannot be detained beyond 24 hours without judicial permission.

### 6. What does Article 11 prohibit?
**Answer:** Slavery and forced labor. Children under 14 cannot work in factories or mines.

### 7. What is Article 19 about?
**Answer:** Freedom of speech and expression, and freedom of the press.

### 8. What three new rights were added by the 18th Amendment?
**Answer:** Right to Information (19-A), Right to Fair Trial (10-A), and Right to Education (25-A).

### 9. What does Article 25 guarantee?
**Answer:** Equality of citizens - all citizens are equal before the law with equal protection, and no discrimination based on sex alone.

### 10. What is Article 25-A about?
**Answer:** Right to free and compulsory education for all children aged 5 to 16 years.

### 11. What are the five types of writs courts can issue to protect fundamental rights?
**Answer:** Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, and Certiorari.

### 12. Can fundamental rights be restricted?
**Answer:** Yes, but only reasonable restrictions for security of state, public order, morality, health, or safety.
