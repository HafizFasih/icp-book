# Constitutional Amendment Procedure

## Introduction

The Constitution of Pakistan (1973) is classified as a **rigid constitution**, meaning it cannot be altered by a simple majority like ordinary laws. The framers intentionally made the amendment process difficult to ensure **political stability** and protect the **rights of the federating units (provinces)**.

The specific procedure for amending the constitution is enshrined in **Part XI, Articles 238 and 239**.

- **Article 238** grants Parliament the power to amend the constitution
- **Article 239** details the specific legislative steps required to do so

This rigidity is a defining feature‚Äîit protects the constitution from arbitrary changes while allowing evolution through consensus-based amendment.

:::success Constitutional Protection
By making amendments difficult, the framers ensured that hasty or partisan changes could not undermine the constitutional framework without broad agreement across political parties and provinces.
:::

---

## The Philosophy Behind Rigid Amendment Procedures

### Why Make Amendments Difficult?

**1. Stability and Predictability:**
- Prevents constitution from being constantly rewritten
- Provides legal certainty and stability
- Allows governance based on established rules

**2. Protection of Minorities:**
- Requires consensus beyond simple majority
- Prevents tyranny of majority from altering fundamental structure
- Protects smaller provinces and minorities

**3. Preserving Federal Character:**
- Requires provincial consent for boundary changes
- Protects federalism as core principle
- Ensures no province can be arbitrarily altered

**4. Democratic Legitimacy:**
- Requires super-majority (two-thirds)
- Demonstrates broad political consensus
- Reflects will of multiple parties and interests

### The Balance:

**Rigid Enough To:**
- Prevent arbitrary changes
- Protect core constitutional values
- Require genuine consensus
- Maintain federalism

**Flexible Enough To:**
- Allow evolution with changed circumstances
- Permit 26 amendments since 1973
- Adapt to new challenges (terrorism, environment)
- Incorporate modern rights (education, information)

## Detailed Procedure for Amendment (Articles 238-239)

The process of amending the Constitution of Pakistan involves the following distinct stages:

### 1. Initiation of the Bill (Article 239-1)

**Who Can Introduce?**
A Constitutional Amendment Bill can originate in **either House** of the Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament):
- **National Assembly** (Lower House)
- **Senate** (Upper House)

**Flexibility:**
Unlike ordinary Money Bills (which must originate in the National Assembly), constitutional amendments have no such restriction. This ensures both houses have equal power in constitutional matters.

**Introduction Process:**
- Bill is formally introduced in the House of origin
- Government or opposition members can introduce
- Typically introduced by government but opposition can also move

:::note Parliamentary Equality
Allowing amendments to originate in either house reflects the equal importance of both chambers in constitutional matters, despite their different compositions.
:::

### 2. Approval by the First House

**The Critical "Two-Thirds Requirement":**

Once introduced, the Constitutional Amendment Bill must be passed by a **two-thirds majority** of the **total membership** of that House.

**Key Point - "Total Membership" Not "Members Present":**

This is a crucial distinction:
- **Not just 2/3 of members present** on the day of voting
- Must be **2/3 of all seats that exist** in the House

**Example:**
- National Assembly has 270 general seats (before reserved seats)
- Two-thirds of 270 = 180 members
- If 150 members are present in parliament, 100 voting yes is NOT enough
- Need 180 members voting yes (even if many are absent)

**Why This Requirement?**
- Prevents snap votes with low attendance
- Requires substantial support, not just present members
- Demonstrates genuine consensus across party lines
- Makes coalition-building necessary

**Calculation for Senate:**
- Senate has 104 seats (23 from each province + 4 from FATA)
- Two-thirds = 70 senators needed
- Same principle applies

### 3. Transmission to the Second House

**Movement to Second House:**
After being passed by the first House with two-thirds majority, the bill is **transmitted to the other House**.

**The Other House's Task:**
The second House also debates the bill and must pass it with the same **two-thirds majority** of its total membership.

**This Ensures:**
- Both houses agree on the amendment
- Smaller provinces (via Senate) have voice
- Larger population (via National Assembly) has voice
- Consensus across both chambers

---

### 4. Handling Disagreements - The Deadlock Scenario

This is a critical aspect of the procedure. There are three possible outcomes:

#### Scenario 1: Passed Without Amendment

**If the second House passes the bill exactly as received:**
- No further action needed in the originating house
- Ready for Presidential assent
- Amendment can proceed to approval stage

**Advantage:**
- Smooth passage when both houses agree

#### Scenario 2: Passed with Amendments

**If the second House introduces changes or modifications:**
- Bill is **sent back to the originating House**
- The originating House must **reconsider the amended version**
- Must pass the **amended bill** by **two-thirds majority**

**Iterative Process:**
- If originating house rejects amendments, bill dies
- If originating house accepts amendments, continues
- Second house must then reconsider if more changes made
- Process continues until agreement reached or stalls

**Complex When Disagreement:**
Multiple rounds possible:
1. First House passes (2/3)
2. Second House amends
3. First House reconsiders
4. Back and forth if continued disagreement

#### Scenario 3: Deadlock - Rejection or Impasse

**If disagreement is fundamental:**
- The second House might **reject the bill entirely**
- Or the **houses cannot reach agreement** despite multiple rounds

**No Joint Sitting Provision:**

**This is crucial:** Unlike ordinary bills where deadlocks can be resolved through a **Joint Sitting** of Parliament (both houses meeting together), **constitutional amendments have NO provision for Joint Sitting**.

**Implication:**
- If the two houses cannot agree, **the amendment bill dies**
- No mechanism to force passage
- Requires genuine agreement between both houses
- Deadlock means the amendment cannot proceed

**Why No Joint Sitting for Amendments?**
- Protects federalism and provincial interests
- Senate (equal provincial representation) cannot be overridden
- Prevents larger house (National Assembly) from dominating
- Requires genuine federal consensus

:::info Federal Safeguard
The absence of a Joint Sitting provision for constitutional amendments is a federal safeguard ensuring that smaller provinces cannot be overridden by the larger National Assembly.
:::

---

### 5. Special Provision: Altering Provincial Limits (Article 239-4)

**A Critical Federal Protection:**

If an amendment bill seeks to **alter the limits (boundaries)** of a province, additional safeguards apply:

**Extra Requirement:**
The amendment bill **cannot be presented to the President** for assent unless it has been **passed by the Provincial Assembly** of that affected province.

**Provincial Assembly Requirement:**
- The Provincial Assembly must pass the bill
- Also requires **two-thirds majority** of its total membership
- This gives the affected province a veto-like power

**Affected Province:**
Any province whose boundaries might be changed must approve:
- Punjab cannot have its boundaries changed without Punjab Assembly consent
- Sindh cannot be altered without Sindh Assembly approval
- Similarly for KPK and Balochistan

**Examples:**
- Redrawing provincial boundaries
- Creating new provinces (e.g., South Punjab proposal would need Punjab Assembly approval)
- Merging provinces (the 25th Amendment merged FATA with KPK, likely required KPK Assembly consent)
- Splitting provinces

**Significance:**
- Protects provincial autonomy
- Prevents center from arbitrarily altering provinces
- Gives local democratic voice in boundary matters
- Reflects federal principle

:::note Federal Principle
This provision exemplifies Pakistan's federal structure‚Äîno major change to provincial identity can happen without provincial participation in decision-making.
:::

---

### 6. Presidential Assent (Article 239-2)

**Final Stage:**

Once the bill has been passed by:
- Both Houses (with two-thirds majorities), AND
- Provincial Assembly (if boundaries affected)

The bill is presented to the **President for assent**.

**Presidential Role:**
Unlike ordinary bills, the **President has NO power to return** a Constitutional Amendment Bill to Parliament for reconsideration.

**What This Means:**
- **The President SHALL assent** to the bill
- Assent is **mandatory and automatic**
- Once both houses have passed it, passage is certain
- President cannot withhold assent
- Cannot send back to parliament for further debate

**Why No Presidential Discretion?**
- Prevents President from blocking democratic decisions
- Constitutional amendments express parliamentary will
- President is ceremonial in parliamentary system
- After 18th Amendment, President has minimal executive power

**Historical Note:**
Even when Presidents had more power, they could not refuse assent to constitutional amendments passed by the required majorities. This reflects the supremacy of Parliament in constitutional matters.

## Comparison: Constitutional Amendment vs. Ordinary Legislation

| Aspect | Ordinary Bill | Constitutional Amendment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Majority Required** | Simple majority (50%+1) | Two-thirds majority |
| **Membership Requirement** | Members present | Total membership |
| **Original House** | Money bills in NA only | Can originate in either house |
| **Second House Deadlock** | Joint Sitting available | No Joint Sitting provision |
| **Presidential Power** | Can return for reconsideration | Must give assent |
| **Provincial Boundary** | Not applicable | Provincial Assembly approval required |
| **Ease of Passage** | Easier | Much harder |
| **Flexibility** | Easy to change | Requires broad consensus |
| **Stability** | Changes frequently | Changes rarely |

## Practical Examples of Amendment Process

### Example 1: The 18th Amendment (2010)

**Proposed by:** PPP government under Asif Ali Zardari
**Passed by:** National Assembly with two-thirds majority (April 8, 2010)
**Passed by:** Senate with two-thirds majority
**Special Check:** No provincial boundaries affected
**Presidential Assent:** Mandatory
**Result:** Became law, removing Article 58(2)(b), abolishing Concurrent List

**Success Factors:**
- All major parties supported (consensus)
- Unanimous passage showed broad agreement
- No provincial objections
- Smooth passage through both houses

### Example 2: The 25th Amendment (2018)

**Proposed by:** Government regarding FATA merger with KPK
**Passed by:** National Assembly with two-thirds majority
**Passed by:** Senate with two-thirds majority
**Special Check:** AFFECTED PROVINCE = KPK
- **KPK Assembly** had to approve (affected by boundary/jurisdictional change)
- **KPK approved** merger with two-thirds majority
**Presidential Assent:** Given
**Result:** FATA formally merged with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa

**Why It Took Longer:**
- Required provincial assembly approval
- Negotiations between federal and provincial governments needed
- KPK government had to agree
- Added step extended timeline

### Example 3: Hypothetical - Creating South Punjab Province

**Proposed by:** Government suggesting South Punjab as separate province
**Would Require:**
1. National Assembly: Two-thirds vote
2. Senate: Two-thirds vote
3. **Punjab Assembly: Two-thirds vote** (because boundary of Punjab altered)
4. Presidential Assent

**Why Difficult:**
- Punjab is largest and most powerful province
- Punjab Assembly likely to oppose (losing territory)
- Needs two-thirds (harder than simple majority)
- Would take multiple years of negotiation

**Historical Note:** South Punjab has been proposed multiple times but not passed because of Punjab Assembly opposition and lack of two-thirds majority in that assembly.

## The Rigidity Index: Pakistan's Amendment Difficulty

Pakistan's amendment procedure is **very rigid**, compared to other democracies:

### Comparative Amendment Difficulty:

| Country | Requirement | Difficulty |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **India** | 2/3 majority in parliament | Rigid |
| **Australia** | Referendum + majority vote | Very Rigid |
| **Pakistan** | 2/3 in both houses + provincial consent (if boundary) | Very Rigid |
| **USA** | 2/3 in Congress + 3/4 states | Very Rigid |
| **UK** | Simple majority | Flexible |
| **Canada** | Varies by section (2/3 to unanimous) | Rigid |

**Pakistan's rigidity** reflects its status as a federal, multi-party state where consensus-building is essential.

## Challenges and Critiques of the Amendment Procedure

### Challenges:

**1. Time-Consuming:**
- Requires passage through both houses
- Possible iterations if disagreement
- Provincial approval adds time
- Can take years to pass major amendments

**2. Promotes Gridlock:**
- Deadlock between houses possible
- Prevents urgent constitutional reforms
- Single provincial opposition can block
- Slows response to crises

**3. Requires Broad Consensus:**
- Difficult to achieve two-thirds majority
- Requires cross-party agreement
- Smaller parties gain leverage
- Can lead to political bargaining

**4. Inflexibility:**
- Cannot respond quickly to changing circumstances
- Stability can become stagnation
- Revolutionary changes not possible constitutionally

### Why These Are Actually Strengths:

**Counter-Arguments:**
- **Stability:** Prevents whimsical constitutional changes
- **Federal Protection:** Prevents majority dominance
- **Democratic Consensus:** Ensures broad agreement
- **Constitutional Preservation:** Protects core values

:::note Balance
The difficulties in amendment are features, not bugs‚Äîthey reflect the framers' belief that constitutional change should require genuine national consensus, not partisan majorities.
:::

## Success Rate of Constitutional Amendments

Since 1973, Pakistan has passed **26 constitutional amendments**, showing the procedure works:

- **Successful Amendments:** 26 (approximately)
- **Failed Attempts:** Multiple (exact number unclear, but several proposals blocked)
- **Average Time:** Varies from months to years

**Why Some Pass and Others Fail:**

**Amendments That Passed:**
- Had broad political support or military backing
- Addressed non-controversial issues
- Didn't threaten provincial interests
- Had clear national consensus (e.g., 18th Amendment)

**Amendments That Failed or Stalled:**
- South Punjab proposal (Punjab assembly opposed)
- Various provincial reorganization proposals
- Some territorial boundary changes
- Controversial ideological amendments

## Conclusion

The constitutional amendment procedure outlined in **Articles 238 and 239** creates a **deliberately rigid process** designed to:

1. **Protect fundamental principles** from easy change
2. **Ensure federal balance** through provincial consent
3. **Require democratic consensus** via two-thirds majorities
4. **Prevent tyranny of majority** through these safeguards

While the procedure is **difficult and time-consuming**, this reflects the framers' wisdom that constitutional changes of this magnitude require **genuine national agreement** rather than simple partisan victories.

The procedure has proven **workable but challenging**‚Äî26 amendments in 50 years represents both successful reform capacity and resistance to frivolous changes. The **18th Amendment (2010)** particularly demonstrated that genuine national consensus can overcome even the rigidity of this procedure.

As Pakistan continues to evolve, the amendment procedure remains the **constitutional gateway for fundamental reforms**, requiring that any major change to the state's structure and principles gain **broad democratic agreement** across all political parties and federating units.

## Flashcards for Quick Review

### 1. What type of constitution is the 1973 Constitution in terms of amendments?
**Answer:** A rigid constitution (difficult to amend).

### 2. What majority is required to amend the Constitution?
**Answer:** Two-thirds majority of total membership in both houses of Parliament.

### 3. In which house can a constitutional amendment bill be introduced?
**Answer:** Either house - National Assembly or Senate.

### 4. What does "two-thirds of total membership" mean?
**Answer:** Two-thirds of all seats that exist in the house, not just members present on voting day.

### 5. If the National Assembly has 270 seats, how many votes are needed to pass an amendment?
**Answer:** 180 votes (two-thirds of 270), even if some members are absent.

### 6. What happens if the two houses disagree on an amendment?
**Answer:** The bill dies - there is no Joint Sitting provision for constitutional amendments.

### 7. What extra step is required if an amendment alters provincial boundaries?
**Answer:** The affected province's assembly must also approve the amendment with a two-thirds majority.

### 8. Can the President refuse to give assent to a constitutional amendment?
**Answer:** No, the President must give assent - it is mandatory and automatic.

### 9. Why is there no Joint Sitting provision for constitutional amendments?
**Answer:** To protect federalism and smaller provinces, ensuring they cannot be overridden by the larger National Assembly.

### 10. How does a constitutional amendment differ from an ordinary bill?
**Answer:** Amendments need two-thirds majority of total membership, can start in either house, have no Joint Sitting option, and President must give assent.

### 11. Which amendment required provincial assembly approval and why?
**Answer:** The 25th Amendment (FATA merger with KPK) required KPK Assembly approval because it affected provincial boundaries.

### 12. Why did the framers make constitutional amendments so difficult?
**Answer:** To ensure stability, protect minorities and smaller provinces, require genuine consensus, and prevent arbitrary changes to the Constitution.
