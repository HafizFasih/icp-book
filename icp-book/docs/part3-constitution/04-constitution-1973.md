# The Constitution of 1973

## Introduction

The **Constitution of 1973** is the supreme law of the Islamic Republic of Pakistan and represents a landmark in the country's political history. It was passed by the National Assembly on **April 10, 1973**, and came into full force on **August 14, 1973**, during the government of **Zulfikar Ali Bhutto**.

Unlike the previous constitutions of 1956 and 1962, the **1973 Constitution was drafted by elected representatives of the people** and enjoys the distinction of being a **consensus document agreed upon by all major political parties of the time**. It provides a comprehensive framework for the governance of the state, balancing modern democratic principles with Islamic ideology.

Most significantly, the **1973 Constitution is still in force today**, making it the only constitution in Pakistan's history to survive for over 50 years. This endurance is a testament to its resilience and legitimacy.

:::success Achievement
The 1973 Constitution stands as Pakistan's most successful constitutional framework, having survived through multiple challenges including military interventions, yet maintaining its core democratic character.
:::

## Salient Features of the 1973 Constitution

The Constitution consists of **280 articles**, **7 schedules**, and a preamble. Its key characteristics are:

### 1. Written and Comprehensive Constitution

- It is a **fully written document** that outlines the structure of the state, powers of various organs of government, and rights of citizens
- **Comprehensive in nature**, detailing every aspect of statecraft to minimize ambiguity and discretion
- More detailed than previous constitutions (280 articles vs. 234 in 1956)
- Includes 7 comprehensive schedules covering various aspects

### 2. Federal System

The constitution establishes a **federal structure** comprising:

- **Four Provinces:**
  - Punjab
  - Sindh
  - Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (formerly NWFP, renamed by 18th Amendment)
  - Balochistan

- **Federal Capital:** Islamabad
- **Federally Administered Areas:** FATA (now merged with KPK via 25th Amendment)

**Distribution of Powers:**
- Powers divided between the center and provinces through two lists
- **Federal List** ‚Üí Powers of the federal government
- **Concurrent List** ‚Üí Powers shared by federal and provincial governments
  - (Abolished by 18th Amendment in 2010, transferring these powers to provinces)
- **Remaining Powers** ‚Üí Powers not mentioned go to provinces

**Federal Character:**
- Provincial autonomy within federal framework
- Provinces retain significant legislative authority
- Recognizes diversity while maintaining federal unity

### 3. Parliamentary Form of Government

The 1973 Constitution returned to the **parliamentary system** after Ayub Khan's presidential experiment.

**Head of Government:**
- The **Prime Minister** is the Head of Government and holds real executive power
- Elected by the National Assembly
- Answerable to Parliament for all actions
- Commands the confidence of the majority in the Assembly

**Head of State:**
- The **President** is the ceremonial Head of State
- Acts on the advice of the Prime Minister
- Limited discretionary powers
- No longer can unilaterally dissolve the assembly (after 18th Amendment removed Article 58-2b)

**Advantages of Parliamentary System:**
- Direct accountability to elected representatives
- Flexibility to remove non-performing governments
- Accommodates diverse political participation
- Checks and balances between institutions

:::note Parliamentary Supremacy
The 1973 Constitution emphasizes that the Prime Minister and cabinet are ultimately answerable to the elected representatives of the people, making Parliament the supreme institution.
:::

### 4. Two-House Legislature

For the first time in Pakistan's history, a **two-house legislature** was introduced to balance representation and ensure federal harmony.

**The National Assembly (Lower House):**
- **Elected on the basis of population**
- Currently has **333 seats** (270 general + 60 reserved for women + 10 for minorities)
- Represents the will of the people directly
- Can pass legislation with simple or special majorities depending on the bill type

**The Senate (Upper House):**
- **Provides equal representation to all four provinces**
- Currently has **104 seats (23 from each province + 4 from FATA)**
- **Each province gets equal voice** regardless of population size
- Ensures smaller provinces are not dominated by larger ones
- Represents federal character of the state

**Why Two Houses?**
The two-house system was designed to:
- Balance population-based and equality-based representation
- Prevent tyranny of the majority
- Protect provincial interests
- Provide a second chamber for deliberation and refinement of laws
- Maintain federal equilibrium

| House | Basis of Representation | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **National Assembly** | **Population** | Reflects people's will; federal government |
| **Senate** | **Equality of Provinces** | Protects provincial interests; federal stability |

### 5. Islamic Provisions

The 1973 Constitution earned the title of an **"Islamic Constitution"** due to its specific provisions:

**State Religion:**
- **Islam is declared the State Religion of Pakistan** (Article 2)
- Affects policies on education, law, and social matters
- Shapes the ideological character of governance

**Sovereignty:**
- Begins with affirmation that **sovereignty over the universe belongs to Almighty Allah alone**
- Authority exercised by people is a sacred trust

**Definition of Muslim:**
- The Constitution clearly **defines a Muslim** for constitutional purposes
- Relevant for determining eligibility for certain posts
- Established through the 2nd Amendment (1974)

**Presidential and PM Qualification:**
- **Both President and Prime Minister must be Muslims**
- Ensures Islamic values guide highest offices of state
- Significant constitutional requirement

**Council of Islamic Ideology:**
- **Established to bring laws in conformity with the Quran and Sunnah**
- Advisory body that reviews proposed legislation
- Recommends changes to align laws with Islamic principles
- Not enforceable but influential in law-making

**Sharia Implementation:**
- States strive to eliminate Riba (interest/usury)
- Article 38 mandates elimination of Riba as early as possible
- Promotes Islamic economic principles

:::info Islamic Democracy
The 1973 Constitution represents a unique model of "Islamic Democracy" where modern democratic institutions operate within the framework of Islamic principles.
:::

### 6. Rigid Constitution

The 1973 Constitution is a **rigid constitution**, meaning it is not easily amendable:

**Amendment Procedure:**
- Requires a **two-thirds majority** in both houses of Parliament
- National Assembly needs 2/3 of **total membership** (not just present members)
- Senate also needs 2/3 of **total membership**
- Both conditions must be met for passage
- Presidential assent required (mandatory for constitutional amendments)

**Purpose of Rigidity:**
- Ensures stability and prevents arbitrary changes
- Protects fundamental structure of the state
- Requires broad consensus before major changes
- Prevents a simple majority from unilaterally altering the constitution

**Flexibility for Amendments:**
- Despite rigidity, 26 amendments have been made since 1973
- Shows constitution is not inflexible but requires serious consideration
- Demonstrates capacity to evolve with changing times

### 7. Independence of Judiciary

The constitution guarantees an **independent judiciary**:

**Structure:**
- **Supreme Court** as the apex court
- **High Courts** in each province (4 High Courts)
- Federal courts and district courts
- Specialized courts for specific matters

**Judicial Powers:**
- Authority to interpret the constitution
- Power to enforce fundamental rights
- Can declare laws unconstitutional
- Final arbiter in disputes between state organs

**Security of Judges:**
- Security of tenure for judges
- Cannot be removed except by formal process
- Separation from executive ensures independence
- Judicial commission involved in appointments (after 18th Amendment)

**Role in Protecting Rights:**
- High Courts can issue writs under Article 199
- Supreme Court can hear cases under Article 184(3)
- Citizens can approach courts to challenge government actions
- Ensures rule of law and protection of fundamental rights

:::success Judicial Independence
The independence of the judiciary has been crucial to the Constitution's durability, allowing courts to check executive power and protect citizens' rights.
:::

### 8. Fundamental Rights

The Constitution guarantees **comprehensive fundamental rights** to all citizens:

**Key Rights Include:**
- **Freedom of speech and expression**
- **Freedom of assembly and association**
- **Freedom of movement**
- **Freedom of religion and worship**
- **Right to property**
- **Right to education** (added by 18th Amendment)
- **Right to fair trial**
- **Right to information** (added by 18th Amendment)
- **Equality before the law**
- **Protection against discrimination**

**Enforcement:**
- These rights are **justiciable** (enforceable by courts)
- Citizens can approach courts if their rights are violated
- Courts can issue writs to protect these rights
- Ensures practical protection, not just theoretical guarantees

### 9. Direct Elections and Single Citizenship

**Direct Elections:**
- Members of National and Provincial Assemblies are **elected directly** by the people
- **Universal adult franchise** (voting age 18, originally 21)
- All adult citizens can vote for their representatives
- Based on one person, one vote principle

**Single Citizenship:**
- The constitution establishes the **principle of single citizenship**
- Citizens are **Pakistanis first**, rather than being identified solely by their provincial residency
- Creates a sense of national unity transcending provincial identities
- Important for maintaining national cohesion

## Constitutional Amendments: Evolution of the 1973 Constitution

The 1973 Constitution has been amended 26 times, showing both stability and adaptability:

### Major Amendments:

| Amendment | Year | Key Change |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **8th** | 1985 | Added Article 58(2)(b) - Presidential power to dissolve Assembly |
| **13th** | 1997 | Removed Article 58(2)(b) - Restored parliamentary supremacy |
| **14th** | 1997 | Article 63A - Floor-crossing laws |
| **17th** | 2003 | Partially restored Article 58(2)(b) under Musharraf |
| **18th** | 2010 | **Landmark reform** - Removed Article 58(2)(b) permanently, abolished Concurrent List, renamed NWFP to KPK |
| **21st** | 2015 | Military Courts for terrorism trials |
| **25th** | 2018 | Merged FATA with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa |
| **26th** | 2024 | Judicial reforms - Special committee for Chief Justice appointment |

The fact that the constitution has survived amendments reflecting changing circumstances demonstrates its **flexibility within rigidity**.

:::note Constitutional Amendment Trend
The pattern of amendments shows Pakistan's political journey: battles between parliamentary supremacy and presidential power (8th, 13th, 17th, 18th amendments), which were largely settled in favor of Parliament by the 18th Amendment.
:::

## Why the 1973 Constitution Has Endured

The 1973 Constitution's longevity can be attributed to several factors:

### 1. Democratic Legitimacy
- Drafted by elected representatives of the people
- Consensus among major political parties
- Reflects popular will and democratic aspirations

### 2. Balanced Federal Structure
- Bicameral legislature balances population and provincial equality
- Four provinces respected as distinct units
- Prevents domination of smaller by larger provinces

### 3. Comprehensive Framework
- 280 articles provide detailed guidance
- Flexibility within structure allows adaptation
- Addresses diverse aspects of governance

### 4. Protection of Rights
- Fundamental rights enforceable by courts
- Citizens have recourse to courts for protection
- Judiciary serves as check on executive power

### 5. Amendment Mechanism
- Rigid enough to protect core structure
- Flexible enough to adapt to changing circumstances
- 26 amendments show capacity for evolution

### 6. Islamic-Democratic Synthesis
- Balances modern democratic governance with Islamic principles
- Satisfies ideological requirement of the state
- Provides ethical framework for laws

---

## Conclusion

The Constitution of 1973 is a **comprehensive, resilient, and enduring document** that has served as the supreme law of Pakistan for over 50 years. Its greatest strength lies in its **federal and parliamentary character**, which accommodates the diverse political and ethnic fabric of Pakistan.

By blending democratic values with Islamic principles, establishing a bicameral legislature to protect provincial interests, and guaranteeing fundamental rights enforceable by courts, the 1973 Constitution remains the **binding force of the federation** and the **guarantor of the rights of the people of Pakistan**.

Despite various political upheavals, military interventions, and constitutional amendments, the **core structure of the 1973 Constitution has remained intact**, testifying to its soundness and the wisdom of its framers. It stands as a model of how a constitution can be both stable and adaptable, rigid and flexible, Islamic and democratic.
